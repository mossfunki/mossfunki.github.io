01 — Context

Does transit proximity raise property values in King County?

Sound Transit’s Link expansion is the largest public infrastructure investment in the Pacific Northwest. This analysis quantifies the price premium that light rail proximity confers on residential parcels — controlling for square footage, age, and Mandatory Housing Affordability zoning tier.

02 — Finding

7% premium for parcels 400–800ft from a station

The hedonic OLS regression identifies a statistically significant price premium (p=0.015) for parcels in the 400–800ft ring around a station. Parcels within 400ft show no premium — likely reflecting noise, density, and commercial adjacency effects.

band_400_800ft: +0.0697 coef (p=0.015)
band_800_1600ft: +0.0434 coef (p=0.108)
SqFtTotLiving: +0.0004 per sq ft (p<0.001)

03 — What This Means

The investment concentrates value in a specific walkable band

Sound Transit’s light rail expansion is creating a measurable price wedge in King County neighborhoods. Homes in the walkable ring around stations are worth roughly 7% more than equivalent homes further out — but the premium disappears within 400 feet of the station itself, where commercial noise and density suppress residential values. For housing affordability planners, this means the transit investment is concentrating value in a specific band, not uniformly across the corridor.

04 — Method

531k parcels, vectorized distance joins, one regression

King County parcel centroids are joined to light rail station points using gpd.sjoin_nearest(), replacing a row-by-row apply loop. Distance bins are one-hot encoded as regression dummies. The model controls for square footage, year built, and MHA zoning tier.

01 — Context

Where are Seattle’s highest-risk pedestrian and bike corridors?

SDOT’s Vision Zero initiative targets locations where pedestrian and bike collisions are most concentrated. This pipeline ingests all recorded ped/bike collisions, clusters them by spatial density, and ranks locations by a composite risk score that accounts for both collision frequency and infrastructure gap.

02 — Finding

Top cluster: 69 collisions, ranked #1 for density & infrastructure gap

The highest-ranked cluster contains 69 pedestrian and bike collisions and sits in a corridor with minimal nearby safety infrastructure. The composite risk score — combining normalized collision count with normalized infrastructure gap — surfaces locations where both dimensions are severe simultaneously.

Cluster #1: 69 collisions — risk score 0.182
Cluster #2: 45 collisions — risk score 0.085
Cluster #3: 13 collisions, large gap — risk score 0.058

03 — What This Means

A prioritized list for where investment saves the most lives per dollar

Seattle’s most dangerous corridor for pedestrians and cyclists — a cluster of 69 collisions near downtown — is also one of the most underserved by existing safety infrastructure. The pipeline scores every cluster on two dimensions: how many collisions it contains, and how little infrastructure surrounds it. That combined score gives SDOT a prioritized list of where targeted investment — protected lanes, signal retiming, traffic calming — would prevent the most harm per dollar spent.

04 — Method

DBSCAN clustering with ball-tree indexing, normalized risk scoring

Raw collision points are filtered to pedestrian/bike involvement, then clustered with DBSCAN (eps=200ft, min_samples=5, algorithm=ball_tree) to avoid O(n²) memory. Both dimensions of the risk score are MinMax-normalized independently before multiplication — preventing cluster size from dominating distance.

01 — Context

Which Seattle census tracts face the highest urban heat island risk?

Urban heat islands disproportionately affect dense, under-canopied neighborhoods — raising health risks for vulnerable populations. This model predicts which King County census tracts are at highest risk using land-use and tree canopy features, then explains the predictions with SHAP values.

02 — Finding

Urban village coverage is the strongest predictor — stronger than tree density

SHAP values show that pct_urban_village has the highest mean absolute impact on model output — nearly twice the contribution of tree density. Permit intensity and median tree diameter follow. New construction rate is nearly zero.

Top feature: pct_urban_village (mean |SHAP| ≈ 0.127)
2nd: tree_density (≈ 0.060)
3rd: median_diameter (≈ 0.049)

03 — What This Means

Seattle’s heat vulnerability is a land-use problem, not just a canopy problem

The model’s most surprising finding: urban village designation — not tree density — is the strongest predictor of heat exposure. Tracts with high urban village coverage are hotter because they’re denser, more impervious, and more built-up. Planting trees helps, but the data suggests Seattle’s heat vulnerability is fundamentally a land-use problem, not just a canopy problem. Climate adaptation planning should target the intersection of urban density and social vulnerability, not just the neighborhoods with the fewest trees.

04 — Method

Tract-level spatial aggregation, Random Forest with stratified CV

Tree, permit, and urban village data are spatially joined to census tracts and aggregated to per-tract density features. The target (top-20% surface temperature anomaly) is derived from an independent UHI layer — no feature variable appears in the target definition, avoiding leakage. SHAP TreeExplainer translates the model into policy-readable importances.
