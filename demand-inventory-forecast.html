Flagship Project — Operations · Supply Chain · Forecasting

Demand Forecasting &
Inventory Optimization

Predicting weekly demand spikes driven by Seattle event schedules and using those forecasts to set dynamic reorder points — reducing stockouts during peaks and excess inventory during slow periods.

Type Portfolio project — simulated data (noted throughout)
Domain Supply Chain · Inventory Planning · Forecasting
Stack Python · pandas · Prophet · XGBoost · Streamlit
Geography Seattle / King County, WA

Problem

Operations teams at Seattle-area retailers and fulfillment centers face recurring demand shocks during major events — Seahawks games, Mariners season, concerts at Climate Pledge Arena, PAX, and Seafair. Static reorder models (fixed reorder points based on average demand) fail in this environment: they generate stockouts during event-driven peaks and excess carrying costs in the quiet weeks between.

The core question: given a known event schedule and historical demand patterns, when should you reorder, and how much? The answer changes week-to-week based on what's coming up. A static reorder point ignores this entirely.

Data

Historical demand (simulated) 104 weeks of weekly unit sales across 5 SKU categories — generated with realistic trend, seasonality, and noise parameters based on BLS retail trade indices
Seattle event calendar (public) Seattle Special Events Office + team schedules — venue, date, expected attendance bucket (5K / 15K / 40K+)
Lead time data (simulated) Supplier lead times per SKU: mean and standard deviation — used for safety stock calculation
WSDOT volume counts (public) Hourly vehicle counts at King County sensor stations — used as a proxy for event-driven foot traffic uplift

Note: demand data is simulated. Event calendar and WSDOT data are real public sources. All code and generation methodology is documented in the repository.

Approach

  1. Baseline decomposition. STL (Seasonal-Trend decomposition using LOESS) applied to 104 weeks of historical demand per SKU. Extracts trend, weekly seasonality, and residual noise — establishes a clean baseline to measure event lift against.
  2. Event feature engineering. Each event encoded as: attendance bucket, venue proximity to distribution center (<5km / 5–15km / >15km), day-of-week, lead time to event, and weather forecast flag (precipitation suppresses foot traffic). Feature matrix built week-by-week.
  3. Demand lift forecasting. Facebook Prophet trained on the baseline time series with event-week dummy regressors added. Outputs a 14-day rolling forecast with 80% and 95% confidence intervals per SKU. XGBoost regression used as a comparison model — evaluated on a held-out 8-week test set using MAPE.
  4. Safety stock recalculation. For each SKU, safety stock is recalculated weekly using: SS = Z × σ_LT × √LT — where Z = 1.65 (95% service level), σ_LT = demand std dev over lead time window, LT = mean supplier lead time in days. Event-week forecasts increase the demand distribution input, raising safety stock automatically ahead of peaks.
  5. Dynamic reorder point. Reorder point set as: ROP = (avg daily demand × LT) + SS. Updated weekly using the forecast rather than historical average — so ROP rises in the two weeks before a major event and returns to baseline after.
  6. Operational output. Streamlit dashboard shows: current inventory position vs. dynamic ROP, 14-day demand forecast with event markers, and a reorder action table (SKU, recommended order quantity, recommended order date). Designed for a non-technical ops manager to act on directly.

The comparison model (static ROP at historical average demand) generated 14 simulated stockout events over the test period. The dynamic forecast-driven model reduced this to 9 — a 36% reduction — at equivalent safety stock investment. Excess inventory carrying cost declined 12% by avoiding over-ordering in non-event weeks.

Business Insight

The key finding: static reorder models systematically under-order before events and over-order after them. The demand signal from the event calendar is available 4–8 weeks in advance — enough lead time to act. The gap isn't in the data, it's in whether the inventory system ingests it.

For a Seattle-area 3PL, retailer, or fulfillment center with exposure to the event calendar (even through secondary effects like increased foot traffic in adjacent zones), this model justifies a direct operational change: replace fixed monthly reorder calculations with a weekly rolling forecast that incorporates known upcoming event load.

Business Value

For Inventory Planners Replace static ROP tables with a weekly updated forecast — acts automatically on event intelligence without manual adjustment
For Operations Managers Clear reorder action table each week — what to order, how much, by when — designed for non-technical users
For Supply Chain Analysts Model is transparent and auditable — all assumptions (Z-score, lead time σ, event lift multipliers) are documented and adjustable
For Finance / Procurement Quantified cost impact: reduced stockout losses vs. reduced carrying costs — gives a basis for capital allocation decisions
